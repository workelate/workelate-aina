The consulting pyramid can't survive agents

Every large consultancy quote you have ever received was priced by a pyramid: one partner, a few managers, and a wide base of junior billable hours. The model is not a conspiracy — it was rational. Enterprise software delivery genuinely needed volume labor: someone had to write the boilerplate, run the test cycles, assemble the documentation, build the decks. Juniors did that work; the margin on their hours funded the firm.

That layer of work — the volume layer — is exactly what AI agents now do. Not the judgment. Not the "should we rebuild dispatch or fix the incentive that breaks it" call. The volume: code generation, test suites, data plumbing, documentation, report assembly. The 80% of delivery hours that were never the reason you hired the firm.

When the base of the pyramid becomes software, what you're left with is the only part that was ever scarce: senior judgment. And senior judgment doesn't need 40 people and 18 months.

The market data already shows the split. Specialist AI-deployment firms are shipping working systems in weeks while enterprise programs run 6–18 months. Buyers at big-firm rates of $300–600 an hour are discovering the partner gives their project a tenth of their time while the average pair of hands on it has three years of experience. Meanwhile, small teams using agent-augmented delivery report two or three engineers producing what eight to ten produced before.

None of this means the big firms die. A global bank running a 40-country core-banking migration still needs an army — coordination at that scale is its own product. But if your business does $5–50M a year, you were never the client that model was built for. You were the client it was priced for.

What this means when you next buy transformation

Ask any bidder three questions. One: who exactly will do the work, by name — not the logo, the people. Two: what will I be able to audit while the work happens — a status deck written by the people being measured, or a log of what was actually done? Three: why is the price what it is — is it a function of the outcome, or of how many people the model requires them to bill?

A pyramid answers: a team, a steering committee, and time-and-materials. An AI-native firm answers: these two people, this log, this fixed number. The second answer wasn't possible five years ago. It is now, and once you've seen it, the first answer sounds like what it is — a business model wearing your project as a costume.
