The consulting pyramid can't survive agents

Every large-firm quote you have ever received was priced by a pyramid: one partner, a few managers, and a wide base of junior billable hours. Accenture, TCS, Infosys, Wipro at the top; DXC, Coforge, Cognizant, LTIMindtree, Mphasis in the tier below; a long tail of product studios and boutiques underneath. Different logos, one shape. The model was not a conspiracy, it was rational: enterprise software genuinely needed volume labor. Someone had to write the boilerplate, run the test cycles, assemble the documentation, build the decks. Juniors did that work, and the margin on their hours funded the firm.

Which means the quote was never really about your outcome. Look at what each side is actually optimising. For the firm, your project is a revenue line, and revenue is a function of how many people they can justify billing for how many months. For you, the pitch is cost saving, we will do it cheaper, offshore it, give you a better rate card. Two parties, and neither one has impact on the table. One is selling headcount, the other is buying a discount. The system it produces is a byproduct.

The pyramid is priced on effort, so it is incentivised to need more effort. That is the equation. We change it: fix the price to the outcome, and effort becomes our problem instead of your invoice.

That base of the pyramid, the volume layer, is exactly what AI agents now do. Not the judgment. Not the "should we rebuild dispatch or fix the incentive that breaks it" call. The volume: code generation, test suites, data plumbing, documentation, report assembly. The 80% of delivery hours that were never the reason you hired anyone. When the base becomes software, what is left is the only part that was ever scarce, senior judgment, and senior judgment does not need forty people and eighteen months.

This is not a story about the giants dying. A bank running a forty-country core-banking migration still needs an army; coordination at that scale is its own product, and Accenture or TCS will sell it to them for years. But if your business does $5–50M a year, you were never the client that model was built for. You were the client it was priced for, a mid-size project run through machinery designed for enterprises, carrying enterprise overhead you will never use. The mid-tier firms and most product studios do not fix this; they run the same pyramid with a thinner partner layer and a cheaper base.

The equation, changed

We built WE_AINA on the opposite arithmetic. The price is fixed to the build, not the hours, so we make money by shipping the outcome faster, not by staffing it longer. Two accountable partners do the judgment; an agent fleet does the volume that used to be a floor of juniors. You pay a third to a quarter of an SI quote, in weeks instead of fiscal years, and you own the code and the data at the end. Our incentive and your outcome point the same direction, which in the pyramid they never can.

So when you next buy a build, ask three questions. Who exactly does the work, by name, the people and not the logo? What can you check while it happens, a status deck written by the people being measured, or the operating numbers moving? And why is the price what it is, a function of your outcome, or of how many people the model needs to bill? A pyramid answers with a team, a steering committee and time-and-materials. We answer with these two people, this fixed number, this working system. Here is exactly how it works.
